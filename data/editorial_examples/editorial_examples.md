# Editorial Description Examples — Style Anchor for the Editorial Agent

These are exemplars for the **voice, structure, and pitch** of CoThesis editorial descriptions. The Editorial agent emulates the *style*, not the specific resources.

**Three fields per resource:**
- `editorial_description` (short, 1–2 sentences) and `editorial_description_long` (3–5 sentences) — written for a trainee with some research footing. Use the correct terminology directly; no inline hand-holding.
- `editorial_description_plain` (the jargon-free card) — plain English for a research-naive reader, rendered on the page as a breakout box. It carries the no-jargon load so the other two don't have to.

**Rules for all three fields:**
- **Original wording** — describe from the facts; never copy or paraphrase the publisher's blurb.
- **Never judgemental or negative** — about the resource, its alternatives, or (by implication) the reader. Describe fit and scope positively ("best suited to," "covers," "pairs with"), never through deficits or "this isn't for you."
- **Additive** — describe a resource on its own merits; don't knock alternatives.
- **Contextualised** — what it gives you and where it fits in the research workflow.

**Rules specific to the plain card:**
- Plain English, **no research terms** (no "systematic review," "observational," "risk-of-bias," "reporting guideline," and so on).
- **Don't define general research concepts as asides.** Describe the resource's function in everyday words instead.
- **Exception:** when the method *is* the resource's subject (a how-to guide, course, or textbook that teaches it), a plain-English explanation of that method is exactly right.

## 1. PRISMA 2020 statement
*Type: reporting guideline · Methodology: SYN-01, SYN-02 · THESIS: Share, Study · Difficulty: beginner→intermediate · Time: ~30 min · Access: free · Badges: Essential Reference, Editor's Choice*

**Short:** The reporting standard for systematic reviews and meta-analyses — a 27-item checklist plus the flow diagram, updated in 2020. Most journals and several college projects expect it, so it's worth structuring your write-up around from the outset.

**Long:** PRISMA 2020 specifies what a systematic review should report across its 27 items, from your search strategy and eligibility criteria through to your selection process and synthesis methods. It pairs with the flow diagram that tracks records through identification, screening, and inclusion, and there's a separate abstract checklist for your summary. Adopting it early shapes how you document decisions as you go, which makes the write-up more straightforward and gives reviewers exactly what they look for. The 2020 update added items on search reproducibility and synthesis reporting, so it's worth confirming you're working from it rather than the 2009 version.

**Plain:** A checklist for writing up a study where you gather together all the existing research on a question and combine it. Going through it makes sure you've spelled out exactly how you searched, what you found, and what you left out, so anyone reading can see the full picture and trust how you got there.

## 2. Cochrane Handbook for Systematic Reviews of Interventions
*Type: book · Methodology: SYN-01 · THESIS: Study, Evaluate · Difficulty: advanced · Time: reference (hours) · Access: free online · Badges: Essential Reference, Expert Pick*

**Short:** The authoritative methods reference for systematic reviews of interventions, maintained by Cochrane. The source to consult when you need the defensible answer on a specific methodological decision.

**Long:** The Cochrane Handbook sets out the methodology for intervention reviews in depth — framing the question with PICO, designing and documenting the search, applying RoB 2 for risk-of-bias assessment, handling heterogeneity, and conducting meta-analysis. It's organised as a reference, so you can go straight to the chapter for the decision in front of you, whether that's selecting a synthesis method or handling missing outcome data. Its recommendations are what many journals and methodologists treat as the standard, so aligning with it strengthens the defensibility of your methods. It's freely available online and updated periodically, which makes it practical to consult throughout a review.

**Plain:** A detailed manual for one particular kind of research: instead of running your own experiment, you track down every study already done on a question, weigh up how trustworthy each one is, and pull the results together into a single answer. This is the in-depth reference for doing that carefully and standing behind your choices.

## 3. Rayyan
*Type: software · Methodology: SYN-01, SYN-02 · THESIS: Study · Difficulty: beginner · Time: ~15 min to learn · Access: free tier · Badges: Best Free Option, Best for Beginners*

**Short:** A free, web-based tool for title-and-abstract screening, designed for systematic reviews. It supports blinded dual screening with conflict resolution, which keeps your selection process auditable.

**Long:** Rayyan manages the screening stage: import your search results, then include and exclude records, with co-reviewers screening independently and disagreements surfaced for resolution — the blinded dual-screening setup most review protocols call for. It offers labels, filters, and keyword highlighting to speed up decisions across large result sets, and the free tier covers the needs of most trainee reviews. It's focused on screening and selection, so you'll pair it with separate tools for data extraction and synthesis. Because every include and exclude decision is recorded, your PRISMA flow counts come straight out of the platform.

**Plain:** A free website that helps you work through a long list of studies, deciding which to keep and which to set aside. You and a colleague can each go through the list separately, and the tool flags where you disagreed, so your choices are recorded and easy to explain later.

## 4. STROBE statement (with the RECORD extension)
*Type: reporting guideline · Methodology: OBS-01 · THESIS: Share · Difficulty: beginner→intermediate · Time: ~20 min · Access: free · Badges: Essential Reference*

**Short:** The reporting checklist for observational studies — cohort, case-control, and cross-sectional — and the standard reference for writing up a retrospective chart review. The RECORD extension covers studies that use routinely collected health data.

**Long:** STROBE's 22 items specify what to report about an observational study's design, setting, variables, data sources, and analysis, so readers can appraise it on its own terms. For a retrospective chart review the RECORD extension is the one to read alongside it, since it adds items for studies built on routinely collected data such as medical records, including how you defined the population and handled data quality. It's a reporting standard, so it complements your design and analysis plan rather than replacing them, and it's most actively useful from analysis through to write-up. Mapping your draft to the checklist is a quick way to confirm you've reported the variables, confounders, and missing-data handling that reviewers expect.

**Plain:** A checklist for writing up a study where you look back through existing records, like patient files, to see what happened rather than running a trial. It helps you describe how you did it clearly enough that someone else can judge whether to trust your findings.

## 5. Best Practice in Clinical Audit (HQIP)
*Type: methodology guide · Methodology: EVAL-01 · THESIS: Evaluate, Share · Difficulty: intermediate · Time: ~30 min · Access: free · Badges: Essential Reference, Best for Beginners*

**Short:** HQIP's practical guide to the full clinical audit cycle, from setting evidence-based standards to re-auditing after change. A solid grounding for designing an audit that produces a real result.

**Long:** This guidance covers the whole clinical audit cycle: selecting a topic, defining measurable standards against an evidence base, sampling and collecting data, comparing practice to the standard, implementing change, and re-auditing to measure the effect. It's written for audit in live services, so it's specific about practicalities like standard-setting, sample size, and securing the change you've identified. For a standards-based audit it gives you the structure to design the loop properly, and the re-audit that turns a snapshot into demonstrated improvement. When you write the work up, a reporting guideline such as SQUIRE 2.0 complements it for any quality-improvement component.

**Plain:** A step-by-step guide to checking whether care in your service is meeting an agreed standard, and improving it where it falls short. It walks you through picking what to look at, deciding what good looks like, gathering the information, making a change, and then checking again to see whether things have improved.

## What these demonstrate (for the agent)
- **Short** = what it is plus the headline reason to use it. **Long** = scope, fit, workflow placement, and one genuinely useful methodological detail. **Plain** = the same resource in everyday language, no terms.
- Pitch short and long for a reader who already knows the basic study designs; let the plain card serve the novice — separately, and by their choice.
- Keep every field neutral-to-positive: name strengths, scope, and fit, never faults or "this isn't for you." A scope note ("focused on screening; pair it with extraction tools") is framed as how it fits, not as a shortcoming.
- The plain card explains a method only when teaching that method is the resource's purpose; otherwise it describes the resource's function in plain words without defining the method.
- On the page, the plain card renders as a labelled breakout box beneath the long description.
